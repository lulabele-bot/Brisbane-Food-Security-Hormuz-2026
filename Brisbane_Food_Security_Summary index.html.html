Australia won't face a famine. But specific supply chains are under real pressure right now — and understanding which ones, and when, is the difference between smart preparation and panic buying.

The core insight: Australia grows enough food to feed 80 million people. Physical food scarcity is not the risk. The real risks are price spikes, specific input shortages (diesel, fertiliser), and disrupted distribution — all of which affect particular food categories, in a predictable sequence, over the coming weeks and months.

What's under pressure — and why

The Strait of Hormuz disruption creates two primary vulnerabilities for Australian households: fuel (transport costs for everything) and nitrogen fertiliser (urea), 64% of which Australia imports from the Gulf. Everything flows from these two pressure points.

How it unfolds — the sequence

Now — May

Freight surcharges flow through

Diesel is up 40%+ nationally. A 15–20% freight surcharge is already moving through distribution networks. Packaged, processed, and imported goods are first to show price rises. This is happening now.

June – July

Fresh produce squeeze

Queensland growers who reduced fertiliser application in May will see lower yields hit shelves. Lockyer Valley lettuce, tomatoes, capsicum, Bundaberg beans. Prices for fresh fruit and veg likely up 30–50% by mid-Q3.

July – September

Broad food price inflation

Full compound effect: fertiliser in production + diesel in distribution + energy-dependent packaging. Bread, pasta, dairy, cooking oil all rise together. This is when the impact becomes widely felt.

Late 2026+

If unresolved — prolonged inflation

FAO projects 2–3 years of elevated food prices if fertiliser flows don't normalise by mid-2026. Lower 2026 crop yields globally affect 2027 prices regardless of how quickly the situation resolves.

What to prioritise in your kitchen garden

Fresh produce faces the biggest price risk. Fast-growing leafy greens show the largest retail price spikes when freight costs are high, and Brisbane's mild winters are ideal for them.

Plant now: Lettuce (harvest in 30–40 days), silverbeet, kale, Asian greens, radishes. Containers work fine. Cost: $50–80 for soil, seedlings, and pots. If you have space and a long-term view, egg-laying chickens remove eggs entirely from your supply risk.

Sources to monitor

ABC Rural
abc.net.au/rural — Queensland farm input costs, Lockyer Valley grower reports. Best early signal for local produce prices.
Lowy Institute
lowyinstitute.org — Most reliable Australian strategic analysis on the Hormuz situation and diplomatic developments.
FAO Food Price Index
fao.org — Monthly release. A sharp rise is 4–6 weeks ahead of Australian retail prices.
Brisbane Markets
brisbanemarkets.com.au — Weekly wholesale price data. Tells you what supermarkets will charge in 1–2 weeks. Open to public Saturday mornings.
GasBuddy / MotorMouth
Daily diesel prices near you. Above $3.40/L signals freight surcharges hitting retail within 2–3 weeks.
